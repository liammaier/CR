Trial 3 - Relative humidity

  1. Relative humidity measures the amount of water vapor in the air. Relative humidity has a large effect on plants because they have large leaf surfaces. Some plants can lose a great deal of moisture and wilt if the air is too dry. On the other hand, some plants are adapted to very dry conditions. Name a plant that you think would like high humidity, and explain why you think so.

Further Investigations

  • Bury your sensor in the dirt of your greenhouse. This is the temperature that the roots of a plant would experience. Is it different from the air temperatures? The roots often love to be warm. How could you make it warmer?

  • Dirt can also make the temperature steadier. Have two groups work together, one measuring the air temperature and the other measuring the dirt temperature. Compare the changes in each one over the course of a day and night.

  • Put other materials in the bottom of your greenhouse, such as sand, rocks, leaves, or open water. See how they affect the air temperature and the relative humidity.

  • Using the container from Trial 1, change the light level by moving the light up and down or by setting your greenhouse in full sunlight. Using both the light sensor and the temperature sensor, explore how the light level affects the temperature.

    37

  • The Greenhouse Effect refers to how changes in the earth's atmosphere affect how much it is heated by the sun. The heating effect is greater if certain gases, such as carbon dioxide, are in the atmosphere. Burning oil and coal increases the carbon dioxide level. There is great concern that as we burn more oil and coal, the earth will heat up.

    Research the Greenhouse Effect on the Web. How is the process like or unlike a real greenhouse? In what ways could our environment change as a result of the Greenhouse Effect?
