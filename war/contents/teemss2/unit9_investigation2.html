Investigation Introduction

Discovery Question: What causes the seasons?

In this investigation you will explore how the earth's motion around the sun causes the changes of the seasons.

1

Thinking About The Question

What causes the seasons?

Look at the following diagram of the earth's yearly path around the sun. It shows the seasons for the Northern Hemisphere, which is the half of the earth north of the equator. Your teacher will assign one student in the class to each of the following positions:

Sun: in the center

Vernal equinox: start of spring

Summer solstice: start of summer and the longest day of the year

Autumnal equinox: start of fall

Winter solstice: start of winter and the shortest day of the year

Months: At least one student will represent each month

Form a circle around the sun. Have the solstices and the equinoxes take their four positions and face in the direction the earth travels (counterclockwise).

Have the months take their positions in order, as in the diagram.

Take turns around the circle. Have each "month" explain its typical weather in the area where you live. Do you see any patterns in the yearly cycle of weather?

Trial 2 - Does the distance from the sun explain the seasons?

  1. To test this question, you will lay out a model of the earth's real orbit. The earth's distance from the sun varies from 147 million kilometers to 152 million kilometers. Your model will have 1 cm representing 1 million km, so the earth's distance will vary from 147 cm to 152 cm.

    Use the CD that was taped on the floor in Trial 1 to represent the sun. Tape down sheets of plain paper in four directions from the CD. Their centers should be about 150 cm from the CD. Use two meter sticks together to measure 150 cm.

  2. Remember that the earth moves counterclockwise around the sun. Draw an arrow on each sheet of paper to indicate which direction the earth is moving, as shown.

  3. On each sheet of paper, mark three distances from the CD: 147 cm, 150 cm, and 152 cm.

    10
  4. On one of the sheets of paper make a bold line through the 152 cm mark. Label it "aphelion" or "farthest distance" and "July 4." On the sheet opposite it make a strong line through the 147 cm mark. Label it "perihelion" or "nearest distance" and "January 4." These are approximate dates. Perhaps you are surprised that the earth is farthest away from the sun on July 4!

  5. On the other two sheets of paper make a bold line through the 150 cm mark. Label them "April" and "October." The four sheets are shown below. You can click on this link to print these sheets for reference.

Trial 3 - Tilted earth

  1. You may have noticed that the axis of the classroom globe is not vertical. It is tilted 23.5 degrees. It is always tilted at the same angle, no matter what time of year.

  2. Use your model globe from Investigation 1. You will also need the compass card you made in Investigation 1. Tilt the globe at 23.5 degrees when you stick it into its clay stand. Cut out a guide for this angle to check if you are tilting it the right amount. Click on this link to download and print the triangle.

  3. Download and print this information about the months, solstices and the equinoxes.

  4. Place the solstice and equinox cards in their proper positions around the orbit. They are close to, but not the same as, the aphelion and perihelion.

    15

  5. Move your model globe around the circle through the seasons. Keep the earth tilted in the same direction all year. Notice that the north pole tilts toward the sun in summer and away from the sun in winter. This is because the sun is in opposite directions from the earth during those seasons. At the south pole, this situation is reversed.

    In spring and fall, the axis is tilted sideways. The angle of the sun is the same at the south pole and the north pole.
