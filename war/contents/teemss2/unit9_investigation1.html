Investigation Introduction

Discovery Question: What are the patterns of the earth's daily cycle?

In this investigation you will explore how the earth's daily rotation around its axis causes day and night and explains the changing pattern of the motion of the sun in the sky.

What are the patterns of the earth's daily cycle?

There is a standard way of locating points on earth, called latitude and longitude.

Latitude measures your distance north or south of the equator. It is measured in degrees. The equator is 0 degrees latitude. The north pole is 90 degrees N, and the south pole is 90 degrees S.

Longitude measures your distance around the earth. It is measured east or west of a line from the north pole to the south pole, through Greenwich, England. It is called the prime meridian, and it is 0 degrees longitude. Longitude circles run from the north pole to the south pole. America is west of England, so the longitudes of places in America have values like 90 degrees W. India is east of England, so the longitudes of places in India are values like 75 degrees E.

Further Investigations

  • Measure how the intensity of reflected sunlight changes as the sun gets higher or lower in the sky. On a piece of stiff white card, at least 20 x 20 cm (8 x 8 in), mount a vertical screw or toothpick about 2 cm (3/4 in) long. Pick a day with a clear sky and bright sunlight. Measure the surface brightness with the light sensor compared to the length of the shadow. As the sun gets lower in the sky, the length of the shadow will increase, so the length of the shadow is a way to measure the height of the sun in the sky. If the light is too bright for the range of your sensor, cover the sensor with layers of masking tape until it does not reach its maximum even when pointed at the brightest reflecting surface.

  • Measure how heat gain of a dark surface changes as the sun gets higher or lower in the sky. To do this, tape a temperature sensor to the back of a dark piece of paper. Move the paper from shadow into full sunlight, each time holding the paper at a different angle to the sun. Note the temperature rise in the first 30 seconds.

  • Measure light and temperature over a 24-hour period. Place a white piece of 8 1/2 in x 11 in paper flat on the ground, and mount the light sensor pointing at it but not casting a shadow. Next to it, place a dark card flat on the ground, with a temperature sensor taped to the underside. Run both data collectors for a full day, and compare them.
