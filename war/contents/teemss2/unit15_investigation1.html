Thinking About The Question

How do you make a measurement you can trust?

Measuring things is a central activity of scientists, because it helps them think about and understand the world. New theories often come about because something new can be measured, or because a measurement becomes more accurate. But measurement comes up everywhere, not just in science: medicine, sports, cooking, games, and building things.

Engineers and manufacturers constantly rely on measurements to make products that are reliable and safe. If the measurements are not reliable, there can be a lot of trouble! In the case of testing the strength of concrete in a bridge, people's lives could be at risk.

Think of an example where measurements are important for things you use every day, and explain the consequences if the measurements aren't correct. Be specific about the measurement that is needed. Be prepared to discuss your thoughts with the class.

Scientists make a distinction between the precision and the accuracy of a measurement. Precision has to do with the repeatability of the measurement and accuracy has to do with the closeness of the measurement to an accepted value. This is a difficult distinction to explain. Here is an example.

2

Suppose you are shooting at a target with a bow and arrow. You aim very carefully. The arrows always hit the target about four inches below the bull's-eye. Your aim is very precise, because you always hit the same spot. But it is not accurate, because you don't hit the bull's-eye. In science, your error would be called a systematic error, because it is always in the same direction.

systematic error, precise (clustered) but not accurate (not near the center)

One day you are tired and rushed. Your arrows hit all over the target. Your aim is neither precise nor accurate. In science, your errors would be called random errors, because they are in many different directions. If you averaged the positions of all the arrows, you might be able to infer where the bull's-eye is, even if you didn't hit it reliably. The average of many measurements can sometimes produce an accurate result.

random errors, not precise (not clustered), individual points not accurate but the average of many points may be accurate.

Finally, one day you figure out that you should always aim a little higher than the center, to allow for the arrow's drop along the way. Then your shot becomes both accurate and precise.

precise (clustered) and accurate (near the center)

3

A real measurement can never be perfectly precise and accurate. For example, measure the width of a tabletop to the nearest centimeter. Does everyone in the class agree? What about to the nearest millimeter? To the nearest tenth of a millimeter?

All measurements include a range of error that indicates how accurate the scientist thinks the measurement is. You should always include this range of error when you report measurements. For example, the generally accepted age of the earth is 4.4 to 4.6 billion years, or 4.5 billion years plus or minus 0.1.

Pick a tabletop in the room and have individuals or students measure its width independently. Record all of the measurements on the board. What is the range of measurements? Record the class average and the range of error.

Trial 2 - Density

  1. Here is one more experiment to try before you invent your own measurement project (Investigation 2). You will determine the density of an object and compare the results of different ways of doing it.

    "Density" is a measure of how much mass there is in a given volume of a material. For example, a cubic meter of steel is heavier than a cubic meter of wood, so steel is denser than wood. A liter of water is heavier than a liter of air, so water is denser than air. In general, gases are less dense than liquids.

    Density is measured as the ratio of mass to volume (called "mass per unit volume"). In this section, it is measured in grams per cubic centimeter or g/cm3.

    A cubic centimeter of water has a mass of one gram. What is its density?

    10 cm3 of aluminum have a mass of 26 g. What is the density of aluminum?

  2. Water is a useful material to compare with other materials, because its density is 1 g/cm3. Also, if a solid material is less dense than water, it will float. If it is more dense, it will sink.

  8. Second, you need to find the volume of your object. You will use three different methods, outlined below. Using different methods to measure the same thing is a common way for scientists to check the accuracy of a measurement.

    1. Method A: Weigh the object when it is fully submerged in water. Objects in water experience a buoyant (upward) force depending on their volume. An object less dense than water will float; an object more dense than water will appear to weigh less. The difference between weight of the object in air and the object in water is equal to the weight of the displaced water. Since 0.01 N is equivalent to 1 g or 1 cm3 of water, you can figure out the volume of water displaced by the object.

Technical Hint - Zeroing the Force Sensor

The force sensor may not read zero when there is no force on it. The software for zeroing the force sensor is not ready yet. For now, when you take a force measurement, subtract the starting force from that measurement to obtain a true reading.

For example, if you lift an object and the force sensor reads 15 N, and it reads 2 N before you have lifted anything, then the true weight of the object is 15 - 2 = 13 N.
