Is it possible to monitor atmospheric conditions to study weather patterns?

Meteorologists study the weather by recording and analyzing data. The term weather refers to the atmospheric conditions around us at any given time. Make a list of different types of atmospheric conditions that affect weather. Remember that a clear day is weather, too. In fact, most meteorologists would call clear days the best kind of weather!

Trial 1 - Building a weather station

  1. Since weather happens outside, you will need to place portable sensors inside a weatherproof box. This type of box is called a Stevenson screen. A Stevenson screen shelters the sensors in a vented box to keep direct sunlight off the temperature sensor and wind away from the relative humidity sensor. If your sensors are protected, you should be able to notice weather patterns that allow meteorologists to forecast weather. Obtain from your teacher a two-liter fruit juice paper carton. Your teacher used a 3 cm x 7 cm template to mark two shutters on each side of the box.

  2. Using a utility knife, your teacher will cut out each shutter so that a flap can be lifted to ventilate your weather station.

    3. 6

  3. Your teacher will cut an additional flap near the top of each lid for ventilation as shown.

  4. Your teacher also used the utility knife to separate the top of the container with a 3 cm lip from the top shoulder as shown.

  5. Place the container and top into a small cardboard box.

    7

  6. Your teacher will spray paint the base and top of the container white to reflect the sun.

  7. Once the container and top have dried, place about 2 cm of sand into the bottom of the container to weigh down the container.

  8. Place the relative humidity sensor and temperature sensor inside the center of the container making sure that they do not touch each other or the sides of the box. If the sensors are attached to a device like a data logger or handheld, place the bottom of the device in the sand so it is secure.

Trial 2 - Selecting a test site

  1. Finding the right place to locate your weather station is extremely important. Since the weather station needs to be level, a flat area is preferred. The weather station also needs to be away from trees and buildings by at least ten times the height of the nearby objects. For example, if you have a tree that is 2.5 m (8ft) high, you need to position the weather station a staggering 25 m (80ft) away from the tree! There are other options, however. You can raise the height of the weather station by using poles or by attaching it to the roof of your school. Ideally the weather station needs to be located above the height of fencing and any other immediate obstacles to enable it to measure wind accurately. This means that the trees or buildings will not act as a shield.

  2. Place the container in a location that is between 1.2 - 2.0 m (4.0 - 6.5 ft) from the ground. Orient the shutters so that they are fully exposed in the North/South direction.

  3. If you have a data logger, you can set it up now to take data. A data logger allows you to take data automatically over many days. It can be disconnected from your computer or handheld and left outside. Ask your teacher if you have a data logger.

Further Investigations

  • If possible, continue to monitor the weather conditions throughout the year. Predict and test the change in temperature and humidity readings for the change in seasons.
  • Many other instruments are often used in weather stations. Unlike a datalogger, some of these need direct exposure to the changing weather conditions. Design and add a rain gauge, windsock, and weather vane to your data logger. How will this increase your ability to forecast the weather?
  • Weather stations often share specific weather information with other stations. Across the globe, more than 10,000 land-based weather stations in 130 nations, plus thousands of ships at sea provide weather data at least four times daily. In the United States, the National Weather Service network collects and provides weather information for aviation, agricultural, and public needs. Contact other schools and compare weather journals and logged data for specific dates. Compare and contrast your weather conditions with those at different locations.
